# LinkedIn Post — The Reframe

**Status:** draft for review. Short-form, clever + plain gloss per voice rule. Roughly 180 words.

**When to post:** after the blog post goes live, so the "read the full argument" link has a destination.

---

## Version A — primary

I ran a full audit of my projects to figure out where I fit in the market. The report came back framed as "Staff AI Engineer."

That wasn't right. So I corrected it:

I don't write any code. I don't design any system. I'm given options by agents and I make selections.

The reframe that came out of the correction is the thing I want to pass along, because I think a lot of senior operators are quietly in the same spot:

Your peer group isn't Staff Engineers. It's architects, directors, and principal consultants — people whose job at that tier has never been to type code. The only difference now is that your execution layer is agents instead of a team of engineers, and your team budget is zero.

That's not a downgrade from IC. It's a different-shaped role. The category is new or newly visible, depending on how you count.

I'd rather test the claim than dress it up.

## Notes on form

- No emojis. No line-break-padding for engagement.
- The clever line is "execution layer is agents, team budget is zero." The plain gloss is "not a downgrade from IC. A different-shaped role."
- Both pair in the same beat, per the short-form feedback rule.
- Do not use the phrase "different-valuable" in the LinkedIn post. It's a useful internal frame but it reads as jargon in public. "Different-shaped role" does the same work without the ring.
- Opening is the audit moment, because LinkedIn posts reward tension in the first line. "I corrected it:" creates the pause.

---

## What NOT to do

- Don't tag anyone in the post. The idea isn't "join my movement" — it's "here's a position I'm testing." Tagging signals the first.
- Don't post it as a carousel, PDF, or image. The whole point of the claim is that it's a plainly-worded position. Formatting tricks weaken it.
- Don't respond to "but how do you know it works?" comments with more argument. Respond with the deployed URL. The blog post is for argument; the comment section is for evidence.
