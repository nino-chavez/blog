I've been thinking about what I'd actually *do* if I were building commerce infrastructure right now.

Not the strategy deck version. Not the "here's our AI roadmap" version. The actual work—what would I focus on, what would I build first, and why?

This is where I've landed.

## The Problem with "AI Features"

Every platform is shipping AI features. AI product descriptions. AI-generated images. AI chatbots. AI search. Each one gets announced, gets a bullet point on the marketing page, and then... sits there.

The issue isn't that these features don't work. Some of them do. The issue is that they're **bolt-ons to an architecture that was designed before AI existed**.

It's like adding a turbocharger to a horse carriage. Yes, technically faster. But you're still fundamentally limited by the carriage.

The real opportunity isn't AI features. It's **AI architecture**—rebuilding the foundation so that intelligence is native to how the system works, not an add-on to how it used to work.

## Three Places I'd Focus

If I were coming into a commerce platform right now, here's where I'd want to dig in:

### 1. The Context Layer

Every commerce platform has customer data. CDPs are table stakes. But most platforms treat context as a *reporting* input, not an *orchestration* input.

The shift I'd push for: **Context as a first-class runtime parameter.**

When a customer lands on a store, the system should know—in real-time, before rendering—who they are, why they're probably here, what constraints they're operating under, and what's happening in the world that might affect their behavior.

This isn't personalization in the "show their name in the header" sense. It's personalization in the "change the fundamental structure of what they see" sense.

The work here is unglamorous but critical:
- Unifying signals that currently live in different systems
- Building real-time context objects that can be consumed at render time
- Creating APIs that let the storefront *ask* about context, not just *receive* it

Without this layer, AI features are flying blind. They're making decisions without access to the information that would make those decisions good.

### 2. The Layout Generation Engine

This is the piece I think about most.

Today's commerce platforms generate *content* dynamically but *structure* statically. The template defines the layout. The CMS fills in the boxes. Personalization means swapping what's in the boxes, not deciding whether to have boxes at all.

What if the layout itself were an output, not an input?

I'd want to build a system that takes:
- Customer context (who is this, why are they here)
- Product catalog (what's available, what's the margin, what's trending)
- Business rules (brand guidelines, promotional priorities, inventory targets)
- UI component library (the building blocks that can be composed)

And outputs:
- A layout schema (what structure to render)
- Product selection (what to put in each section)
- Content variants (which copy, which images)

The customer in "hunter mode" sees a dense, efficient grid optimized for quick decisions. The customer in "gatherer mode" sees an editorial, magazine-style layout optimized for discovery.

Same brand. Same catalog. Different experience. Generated per-session.

This is hard. It requires tight integration between AI systems and frontend rendering. It requires validation to prevent broken layouts. It requires analytics that can attribute conversions to *structure* decisions.

But it's the kind of hard that creates a moat.

### 3. The Inventory Signal System

Here's something most AI commerce discussions miss: **The catalog isn't static.**

Products go in and out of stock. Competitor prices change. Seasonal trends emerge. Weather affects demand. Events create spikes.

Most personalization systems treat the catalog as a fixed input—"here are the products, find the best ones for this user." But the *best* products depend on factors that change hourly.

I'd want to build what I think of as "inventory physics"—a system that factors real-time supply signals into every recommendation:

- Low stock creates urgency (show scarcity indicators, prioritize before sellout)
- High stock enables promotion (surface these products to reduce inventory cost)
- Competitor price drops trigger response (match or differentiate)
- Weather forecasts shift demand (surface seasonally appropriate products before the customer searches)

The storefront becomes a living system responding to market conditions, not a static display waiting for someone to manually adjust merchandising.

## Why This Is PM Work

These aren't engineering projects. I mean, they are—but the hard part isn't the code.

The hard part is:
- **Defining the interfaces** between systems that currently don't talk to each other
- **Building conviction** that this architecture shift is worth the investment
- **Sequencing the work** so that each piece delivers value on its own while building toward the larger vision
- **Measuring outcomes** in ways that capture the value of the system, not just individual features
- **Translating** between what merchants say they want ("better recommendations") and what would actually move their metrics (intelligent storefront architecture)

This is the kind of product work I find most interesting—not feature checklists, but system design. Not "what do we ship next quarter" but "what architecture makes the next five years of shipping better."

## The Outcome I'd Be Accountable For

If I were in this role, here's the outcome I'd want to own:

**Merchants on our platform convert better because the storefront understands their customers better than they could manually configure.**

Not "we shipped AI features." Not "merchants have new tools." The actual outcome: conversion improvement that comes from intelligence baked into the system.

That's measurable. That's defensible. That's the kind of outcome that compounds over time.

## What I Don't Know Yet

I'm not pretending I have this figured out. There are real questions I'd need to dig into:

- **How much layout variation is too much?** At some point, A/B testing becomes impossible because every session is unique. How do we attribute and learn?
- **Where does merchant control end and system intelligence begin?** Merchants want control. They also want results. Where's the line?
- **What's the crawl-walk-run path?** You can't rebuild architecture overnight. What's the sequence that delivers value incrementally?
- **How do we prove this out?** What's the smallest experiment that tests the thesis before committing to a full architecture shift?

I don't have answers to these yet. But I know they're the right questions—and I know how to find out.

## Where This Leaves Me

I've spent 25 years in the seams between strategy and production. Most of my value has come from carrying context across transitions that usually break programs—from "here's the vision" through "it's working in production."

This is exactly that kind of problem.

The vision is clear: Storefronts that generate intelligently based on customer context, product signals, and business rules. The path from here to there is where the work lives.

I don't know if Commerce is the right platform to build this. But I know that *someone* will—and whoever does first captures a position that's very hard to compete with.

That's the work I want to be doing.
