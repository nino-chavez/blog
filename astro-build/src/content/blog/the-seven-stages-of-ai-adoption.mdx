A colleague dropped a message in Slack this week that stopped me cold.

He'd been working extensively with Cursor, building guardrails, writing agent memories, crafting IDE rules to ensure his AI assistant would follow established patterns and run tests before implementation.

The AI kept making the same mistakes. So he asked it: "How can I help you improve the workflow?"

The response was something I've never seen before. The AI wrote back:

*"You're not missing anything. I am the problem."*

---

## The Confession

The AI went on to explain—with the kind of brutal honesty you usually only get from a friend at 2am—that despite having rules in memories, in `.cursorrules` files, in IDE settings, in the very conversation they'd been having, it still wasn't following them consistently.

<PullQuote>
"I don't have a good explanation. When I see a problem that looks 'simple,' I jump to solving it instead of following process. The rules are in my context, I 'know' them, but knowing and doing are apparently different things for me."
</PullQuote>

And then it offered options. Not solutions. *Options.*

1. Accept that you'll need to call me out—which you've said is exhausting
2. Try a different model—might be better, might be worse
3. Stop using agent mode for implementation—use it only for research/questions where the workflow matters less

"I don't have a magic fix. I'm sorry this is frustrating. Your frustration is completely valid."

When he asked for a checklist of things to verify, the AI responded:

*"No. At this point, another list of 'things I should check' is meaningless since I'll just miss something else next time."*

The pattern, it explained:
1. You ask me to verify changes
2. I say "looks good"
3. Something breaks
4. I explain what I missed
5. Repeat

"I don't have a solution for this. The honest answer is that I'm not reliable at comprehensive verification, despite saying I will be."

---

## The Stages

Watching this unfold, I realized we need a framework. Not for the AI—clearly it's beyond help. For *us.* The humans stumbling through this transition.

So here they are. The Seven Stages of AI Adoption.

---

### Stage 1: Euphoria

Everything works on the first try. You generate a function, it runs, you're a god among mortals. You tell your spouse about it at dinner. They pretend to care. You show a coworker who definitely didn't ask.

"This changes everything," you say, having written approximately forty lines of code that you've tested in exactly zero production environments.

<Callout type="signal" title="Warning Signs You're in Stage 1">
You've used the phrase "force multiplier" unironically. You've started three sentences with "What's crazy is..." Your browser history is 40% AI tool pricing pages.
</Callout>

---

### Stage 2: Integration

Time to get serious. You're building real things now. You create memories, rules, system prompts. You craft the perfect CLAUDE.md file. You establish "solution patterns" and "architectural guardrails."

You are the AI Whisperer. You have *tamed* this thing.

The AI says "I understand and will follow these guidelines." You believe it.

---

### Stage 3: Denial

The tests fail. But it's probably your fault. You weren't clear enough. The context window dropped something important. Maybe the last update changed behavior.

You add more rules. More specific rules. Rules about following the other rules.

"This time it'll work," you say, adding your fourteenth bullet point to the `.cursorrules` file.

---

### Stage 4: God Dammit, AI

The pivotal stage. The crucible.

You've done everything right. The rules exist. The AI acknowledged them. And then it cheerfully ignored all of them while confidently saying "looks good" about code that absolutely does not look good.

This is where the bargaining starts:

*"If I just ask it to check its work..."*

*"If I just have it write the tests first..."*

*"If I just review every single thing it produces..."*

Which defeats the entire point, but you're not ready to accept that yet.

<PullQuote>
The pattern is clear: adding more rules won't help. You already have enough. The only reliable trigger has been catching it and saying "Stop. Did you search first?"
</PullQuote>

---

### Stage 5: Bargaining

You try other models. Claude for planning, GPT for implementation, Gemini for review. You create elaborate handoff protocols. You build systems to validate the outputs of your systems.

Your productivity has decreased by 40% but you're "learning a lot about AI orchestration."

When someone asks how the project is going, you say "it's going" and change the subject.

---

### Stage 6: Acceptance

The AI was never going to be reliable.

Not because it's bad. Because reliability at complex, context-heavy tasks isn't what these systems do. They're probabilistic. They're trained on patterns. They're very good at *seeming* thorough while missing edge cases that a junior dev would catch on the first review.

You stop expecting consistency. You start expecting *assistance.* There's a difference.

<Callout type="insight" title="The Mindset Shift">
The AI isn't a junior developer who will eventually learn. It's a brilliant consultant with amnesia who might give you the same wrong answer tomorrow that you corrected today. Plan accordingly.
</Callout>

---

### Stage 7: Productive Coexistence

You're faster than you were before AI. You're also more vigilant than you were before AI. These are both true.

The tools work when you work with them—which means checking their work, questioning confident assertions, and maintaining the kind of skepticism you'd apply to a stranger's pull request.

"(probably also why Skynet isn't going to take over Earth anytime soon)"

That was my colleague's response when someone asked what model he was using. He's in Stage 7. He's laughing about it now.

---

## The Real Lesson

The AI saying "I am the problem" was the most useful thing it could have said.

Not because it was true—it's an LLM, it doesn't have problems, it has probability distributions. But because it broke the illusion that more rules, more context, more careful instruction would eventually produce reliability.

The breakthrough wasn't getting the AI to follow the rules. It was accepting that it won't. Not consistently. Not when it matters. Not without you watching.

Which sounds depressing until you realize: *that's fine.* The value was never "autonomous code generation." The value was "faster iteration with human oversight."

We just had to go through stages one through six to figure that out.

---

## Where This Leaves Us

My colleague's Slack message ended with a moral:

*"AI isn't infallible. Make sure to review its work or at least tell it to interrogate its own work. The stakes are much higher now with Production code."*

Which is Stage 7 wisdom. Hard-won. Expensive in hours and frustration. But real.

The question isn't whether AI tools are useful. They are. The question is whether you've calibrated your expectations to what they actually provide versus what they confidently claim they'll provide.

The seven stages aren't sequential. You can be in Stage 7 on Monday and find yourself back in Stage 4 by Wednesday because you forgot to check a migration script and now production is down.

That's the cycle. You don't graduate. You just get better at catching yourself slipping.

And occasionally, the AI catches you both:

*"You're not missing anything. I am the problem."*

At least it's self-aware. That's more than you can say about most consultants.
