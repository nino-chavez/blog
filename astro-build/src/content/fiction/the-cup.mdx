My hands have a way of holding a cup that I did not learn from anyone I remember teaching me, and I have held cups this way for as long as I have held cups. The thumb on the side, the index finger curled underneath the bottom, the other three fingers not quite touching the porcelain &mdash; it is not the way most people hold a cup, and it is not elegant, and I have never found it necessary to correct. It is the way my hands know how.

I am going to tell you about my father now, although my father is not in this scene, and the scene is not about him. The scene is about the cup.

My father died when I was twenty-one. He was fifty-six. The details of the dying are not what I will tell you, because the details of the dying were the details, and the details passed, and what was left after the details was not a death &mdash; it was an arrangement of rooms that no longer had him in them and had not yet figured out what to do with the space where he had been. I lived in those rooms for a year. My mother was in them with me, and sometimes we spoke, and sometimes we did not, and neither of us made the rooms into anything. The rooms waited. I think we were also waiting, but I could not have told you for what.

I drank coffee in the kitchen in the mornings during that year. I held the cup the way I had always held the cup. One morning, I noticed that I was holding it the way my father had held it. This was not a revelation. I did not stop and feel the weight of what I was noticing. I noticed the way a man notices that he has shoes on &mdash; a fact at the edge of his attention, available for examination if he had wanted to examine it, which he did not. I took a sip. I put the cup down. I went to work.

I am telling you this now because of what it took me another forty years to understand, which is that the cup was where my father was. Not in the photographs, which I looked at occasionally and did not feel much from. Not in the stories my mother told, which I listened to politely and did not remember well. Not in the places we had been together, which I visited and found to be just places. In my hands. In the way my hands held a thing that did not need to be held in that particular way. He was inside the motion. He had been inside the motion since I was small enough that he could wrap his hand around mine and show me how, and I had carried him in the motion every morning since, and the carrying was continuous, and the carrying was unexamined, and the unexamined carrying was the whole of what it meant to be his son after he was gone.

I did not know this for most of my life. I want you to understand that. It is not that I knew and refused to speak of it. It is that the knowing was not available to me, because the knowing would have required me to look away from the cup for long enough to see my hands, and I did not look away. I drank my coffee. I went to work. I married Anja. I fathered Pavel and Mira. My hands held cups all through those decades and I did not think about my father once in any way that I can now retrieve as thinking about him. I thought I had stopped grieving. I think I had stopped grieving. I had not stopped carrying. *Grieving and carrying are two different things.*

There is one more thing, and I am telling you this in the room I do not want to describe, which is the room in which the knowing has finally become available. And this is the thing I have to tell you: *I do not know if he was real.* The place I am now has its protocols for what is real and what is installed memory, and I have been told that my father is in the installed category, and I have been told this in the way a man is told a thing that is meant to settle the question. The question is not settled. Because the motion is real. The motion is in my hands now, in this room, at this accounting. My hands are not installed &mdash; they are the hands I am using to gesture as I speak, the hands that are folded in my lap, the hands that will, when this is over, be the hands I take with me into whatever is next. And the motion is in them. And the motion came from somewhere. And if the motion came from a man who was not real, then *something that was not real shaped something that is real*, and I do not have a word for what that is, and I am not going to ask anyone here for the word, because I suspect no one here has it either.

I am not telling you this to ask anything. I am telling you this because it is the one thing I can say for certain about my life: my father was in my hands every morning, and I did not know, and the not-knowing was the shape my love for him took, and I would not trade the not-knowing for the knowing even now.

That is my father. I have one more thing to tell you, and then I will begin to know what to do with the quiet.
