## What they do well

- **Author-as-catalog above the fold.** Home leads with "Robin Sloan, a creative industrialist" — role-and-identity-first, then a list of major publications (three novels, novellas, short stories, essays, special projects, commercial ventures). The home page IS the catalog, not the front door to the catalog.
- **Two-newsletter split for two audiences.** Main newsletter goes "every 29 ½ days" (irregular but deliberate); separate "Lab newsletter" for technical readers. The split acknowledges that one audience can't carry two cadences — better to fork than dilute.
- **Cadence as voice signal.** "Every 29 ½ days" is intentional, not lazy. The half-day surplus signals deliberate-irregular over either weekly-grind or whenever-I-feel-like-it. The cadence promise IS a character statement.
- **Buried subscribe affordance.** No prominent CTA; the newsletter is mentioned in the catalog but not pushed. Subscribing requires intent. Filters for high-signal readers and matches the hiring-evaluator persona's no-marketing-tells rule almost exactly.
- **Commercial ventures in the same nav.** Olive oil company and print shop sit alongside fiction and essays. The commercial surfaces don't degrade the literary ones because they're presented as part of the same author's range, not as monetization.

## What we could learn

- **D4 (cadence promise) can be expressive, not just calendrical.** "Every 29 ½ days" tells readers more about Sloan's voice than "weekly" or "monthly" would. v2's cadence promise (D4) could use the same — a phrase that signals intent (e.g., "when the argument is ready," "Dispatch #N drops at the end of each thread") rather than a generic interval.
- **Author-as-catalog as a Thesis A home-page shape.** If Nino picks Thesis A (positioning realignment, v2 = re-brand of v1), Sloan's home is closer reference than any peer in the v1 set. Catalog-shape home pages work when the reader's question is "what does this person make?" — exactly the hiring-evaluator's question.
- **Cross-format coexistence without segmentation.** v1 has fiction (10 items) alongside architecture posts. Sloan's site shows this is workable — the format-typing carries the signal, the visual treatment respects each, and the catalog framing lets the reader pick their own thread. Supports D3 fiction-decision = "keep, don't spin to separate site" if Nino wants that path.

## What to avoid

- **The catalog-as-home shape only works for established work.** Sloan has three novels published; the catalog feels substantial because there's substantial work. v2's home page can't lead with a catalog the same way until the v2 corpus has its own bulk — v1 corpus migration (per Stage 5 migration plan) needs to land first.
- **"Creative industrialist" is a specific positioning Nino doesn't share.** Sloan's role-label fits because his work spans fiction + commerce + tech in a deliberate way. Nino's "architect-who-directs-agents" frame is more narrow; don't borrow Sloan's expansive descriptor shape.

## Implications for v2

- **D4 (cadence promise) framing reference** — Sloan's "every 29 ½ days" is the cleanest example of cadence-as-voice in the cross-industry set. Whatever v2 picks, the answer should pass the test "does this signal something about how this publication thinks" — not just "what's the schedule."
- **Author-as-catalog home page is Thesis A's natural shape** — if D1 lands as A, the home design should reference Sloan over Stratechery. Update Stage 3 home page sketch to include this as a third [Thesis A — author-as-catalog] variant.
- **Cross-format coexistence is viable for D3** — supports the "keep fiction in v2" option without requiring a separate site. The cost is design-treatment work to make formats visually distinguishable.
